Abstract

Universal control policies promise a scalable alternative to the costly training of robot-specific controllers. Recent works have demonstrated that proper conditioning on robot morphological context is key to generalizing across robot embodiments and to obtaining good performance on this challenging multi-task control problem. To provide the morphological context of the robots’ 3D configuration to the control policy, existing methods have posed the strong assumption that robots are composed of simplified geometric primitives and have built the context tokens using these primitives’ one-dimensional parameter vectors. In this paper, we relax this assumption by proposing volumetric morphology encodings (VME). These novel morphology tokens are learned during a pretraining phase from a 3D robot morphology dataset that we collect using tools from robotics and computer vision such as multi-view depth fusion. We train VME-conditioned universal controllers on the challenging UNIMAL benchmark, and we find that our approach achieves superior generalization, with substantial improvement over the state-of-the-art on difficult tasks (up to 60% on the hardest task that mixes manipulation and locomotion). Furthermore, our approach demonstrates superior zero-shot transfer to novel embodiments.

Key Contributions

  • Morphology Context Analysis: We empirically demonstrate that 3D body plan context is substantially more important than dynamics context for universal control performance.
  • Volumetric Morphology Encodings (VME): We propose a novel representation for robot body plans that leverages multi-view depth fusion to capture 3D shape and connectivity, learned offline using a volumetric CNN autoencoder.
  • State-of-the-Art Results: VME dramatically improves universal control performance by up to 60% on the hardest UNIMAL benchmark tasks and enhances zero-shot transfer to novel robot embodiments.

Method Overview

Prior work on universal control has provided morphological context by assuming robots are composed of primitive shapes (spheres, capsules, etc.) and feeding their parameter vectors to the policy. This approach is limiting because: (1) it precludes working with real robots that are rarely composed of primitives, and (2) it doesn't leverage 3D equivariances.

We propose to learn morphology tokens from actual 3D data. Using multi-view depth fusion—a technique commonly used in robotics for room scanning—we turn the process inward to scan robot body plans. We then train a volumetric CNN autoencoder to compress each robot limb's 3D scan into a low dimensional latent space (L=16) that serves as the morphology context token.

Results

On the standardized UNIMAL tasks, VME-conditioned policies achieve improvements of up to 60%, with particularly high gains on harder tasks. We also observe improved zero-shot transfer to previously unseen robot morphologies.
